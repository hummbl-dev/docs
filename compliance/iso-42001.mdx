ISO 42001:2023 is the international standard for AI management systems, published by ISO in November 2023. It defines requirements for establishing, implementing, maintaining, and continually improving an AI management system (AIMS) within an organization. Unlike the EU AI Act, ISO 42001 does not target specific use cases — it applies to any organization that develops, provides, or uses AI systems and wants to demonstrate systematic governance to stakeholders. Certification is performed by accredited third-party bodies, the same way ISO 27001 works for information security. The `hummbl-governance` package provides the operational controls that map to ISO 42001's technical and documented-information requirements.

## Why ISO 42001 Matters for Your Business

ISO 42001 is increasingly referenced in enterprise procurement questionnaires, particularly in financial services, insurance, and healthcare. Buyers who already require ISO 27001 are beginning to add ISO 42001 as a condition of vendor onboarding. It also pairs naturally with SOC 2 Type II: your SOC 2 auditor covers availability and processing integrity; your ISO 42001 assessor covers AI-specific risk management and operational control. Together they cover the governance surface area that enterprise security teams want to see.

<Warning>

## Key Clause Mapping

ISO 42001 is organized around the Plan-Do-Check-Act structure common to all ISO management system standards. The clauses with direct technical control requirements map to specific Hummbl primitives.

| ISO 42001 Clause | Requirement | Hummbl Control |
|-----------------|-------------|----------------|
| 6.1 | Actions to address risks and opportunities | Circuit Breaker + Kill Switch |
| 7.5 | Documented information | Governance Bus (JSONL records) |
| 8.4 | AI system impact assessment | Delegation Tokens (scope evidence) |
| 9.1 | Monitoring and measurement | Governance Bus + `BusWriter.read_since()` |
| 10.2 | Nonconformity and corrective action | Kill Switch (`EMERGENCY` mode) |

### Clause 6.1: Risks and Opportunities

Clause 6.1 requires that your organization determine risks and opportunities relevant to its AI systems and plan actions to address them. This is not a one-time risk assessment — ISO 42001 requires that the risk management process be operational and continuous. The Circuit Breaker provides continuous per-adapter risk monitoring, tracking failure rates in real time and isolating adapters that exceed defined thresholds. The Kill Switch provides escalating response actions that correspond to different risk levels, from suspending non-critical operations to triggering an immediate emergency halt.

### Clause 7.5: Documented Information

Clause 7.5 requires that documented information required by the management system be created, updated, controlled, and retained. For an AI system, this includes records of decisions, actions, incidents, and the context in which they occurred. The Governance Bus produces this documentation automatically — every event posted to the bus is appended to a JSONL file with a timestamp, agent identity, event type, payload hash, and HMAC signature. No manual documentation step is required; the records are a byproduct of governance-instrumented operation.

### Clause 8.4: AI System Impact Assessment

Clause 8.4 requires that your organization assess the impacts of AI systems on individuals and society. Delegation Tokens provide scope evidence for this assessment: each token records the capabilities granted to an agent, the grantor's identity, the grantee's identity, and the expiry time. When you need to demonstrate what any agent was authorized to do at any point in time, your delegation token history gives you a complete, cryptographically verifiable record of authorized scope.

### Clause 9.1: Monitoring and Measurement

Clause 9.1 requires systematic monitoring of AI system performance against defined criteria. `BusWriter.read_since()` gives you a time-windowed query over your audit log — you can retrieve all governance events from the last hour, the last deployment, or any arbitrary time range. Combine this with the Circuit Breaker's failure rate metrics and you have both qualitative (what decisions were made) and quantitative (how reliable were the adapters) monitoring evidence.

### Clause 10.2: Nonconformity and Corrective Action

Clause 10.2 requires that when a nonconformity occurs, your organization take action to control and correct it, evaluate the cause, and implement corrective action to prevent recurrence. The Kill Switch's `EMERGENCY` mode provides the immediate containment action. The Governance Bus provides the incident record — every event leading up to and including the halt is in the JSONL log, available for root cause analysis. The combination satisfies both the response requirement and the documented-information requirement for corrective action.

## Portable Evidence: Why JSONL Beats a SaaS Dashboard for ISO Auditors

ISO 42001 auditors work the same way ISO 27001 auditors do: they request evidence, review it, and verify that it demonstrates conformity with the standard. The evidence review happens in a controlled assessment environment — not inside a vendor's cloud console.

Consider what happens at audit time with each approach:

**SaaS governance dashboard:**
- Auditor must trust screenshots you export
- Chain of custody is unverifiable — you could have edited a screenshot
- Auditor cannot run queries against your data
- If the vendor's service is unavailable, your evidence is inaccessible
- Regulated buyers may be prohibited from sharing data with the SaaS vendor at all

**Hummbl JSONL audit log:**
- Auditor receives a file — text editor, grep, and standard tools work immediately
- HMAC signatures are independently verifiable without Hummbl installed
- Auditor runs their own queries to verify completeness and integrity
- Evidence is in your custody and never left your infrastructure
- Works in air-gapped, IL4/IL5, and restricted-perimeter environments

The audit log is yours. It lives in your infrastructure. An auditor can sit down with it and work through it without any vendor involvement.

## Generating an ISO 42001-Compliant Incident Record

When a nonconformity occurs, you need to produce a structured incident record that captures the triggering event, the response action, and the evidence chain. The following example shows how to generate this record using `BusWriter` at the moment of detection.

```python
from hummbl_governance import BusWriter, BusEvent, KillSwitch, KillSwitchMode
import datetime

bus = BusWriter(log_path="./governance.jsonl", signing_key=YOUR_SIGNING_KEY)
ks = KillSwitch()

def handle_nonconformity(
    agent_id: str,
    detected_violation: dict,
    severity: str,
) -> None:
    """
    Record a nonconformity event and invoke the appropriate halt mode.
    Satisfies ISO 42001 Clause 10.2 (nonconformity and corrective action).
    """
    # Record the detected nonconformity to the bus (Clause 7.5 documented information)
    bus.post(BusEvent(
        agent_id=agent_id,
        event_type="nonconformity_detected",
        payload={
            "violation_type": detected_violation["type"],
            "violation_detail": detected_violation["detail"],
            "severity": severity,
            "detected_at": datetime.datetime.utcnow().isoformat() + "Z",
            "policy_ref": detected_violation.get("policy_ref"),
        },
    ))

    # Select the appropriate halt mode based on severity (Clause 6.1 risk response)
    if severity == "critical":
        halt_mode = KillSwitchMode.EMERGENCY
    elif severity == "high":
        halt_mode = KillSwitchMode.HALT_ALL
    else:
        halt_mode = KillSwitchMode.HALT_NONCRITICAL

    # Invoke the kill switch (Clause 10.2 corrective action — containment)
    ks.halt(halt_mode)

    # Record the corrective action taken (Clause 10.2 documented information)
    bus.post(BusEvent(
        agent_id=agent_id,
        event_type="corrective_action_initiated",
        payload={
            "halt_mode": halt_mode.value,
            "initiated_at": datetime.datetime.utcnow().isoformat() + "Z",
            "triggered_by": "nonconformity_detected",
        },
    ))
```

The resulting JSONL entries form a complete, timestamped incident record — detection event followed immediately by containment action — that satisfies Clause 10.2's documentation requirement without any manual steps.

```json
{"ts":"2025-11-14T14:05:22.001Z","agent_id":"underwriting-agent-v2","event_type":"nonconformity_detected","payload":{"violation_type":"unauthorized_data_access","violation_detail":"Attempted read of out-of-scope PII field","severity":"critical","detected_at":"2025-11-14T14:05:22.001Z","policy_ref":"data-access-policy-v3"}}
{"ts":"2025-11-14T14:05:22.003Z","agent_id":"underwriting-agent-v2","event_type":"corrective_action_initiated","payload":{"halt_mode":"EMERGENCY","initiated_at":"2025-11-14T14:05:22.003Z","triggered_by":"nonconformity_detected"}}
```

Two milliseconds between detection and containment. Both records are HMAC-signed and auditor-verifiable without any Hummbl tooling installed.
